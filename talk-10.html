Abstract

Protein Kinases (PKs) have become one of the most important drug targets in the 21 st century. PKs catalyze the transfer of the γ-phosphate group of ATP to a substrate protein, a critical modification for regulating cellular signaling. Due to their central role in tumor cell proliferation, cell survival, and therapy resistance, they are one of the most attractive cancer drug targets. Several kinases have been clinically validated for cancer therapy. To date, there are 68 FDA-approved small-molecule kinase inhibitors for various diseases including solid and nonsolid tumors or both. The human kinase superfamily is mainly grouped into two classes viz., the eukaryotic protein kinases (ePKs) and atypical protein kinases (aPKs). Earlier majority of the cancer drug discovery efforts were focused on ePKs, however, after the discovery of idelalisib in 2014, the focus has shifted towards aPKs. Though aPKs share a common archetypical kinase fold, it lacks highly conserved eukaryotic kinase motifs (instead have mirrored catalytic HRD motif) and holds altered hydrophobic spines. These sequence/structure differences offer a unique opportunity for the discovery of next-generation kinase inhibitors on aPKs. After careful target evaluation, both ePK and aPK targets were selected for further studies. We employed computational chemistry methods, structure-based and ligand-based drug designing for screening novel kinase inhibitors. The experience and learnings from ePK ligand chemical space explorations were used to navigate our drug-designing protocol for obtaining more selective and potent aPK inhibitors. Overall, our talk summarizes the case studies on both of the above-mentioned kinase targets, and beyond.
